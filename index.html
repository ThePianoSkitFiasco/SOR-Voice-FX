Settings
Crunchiness
The original game stored voice audio with very low "bit depth" — basically how many steps the audio could use. Lower = grittier and more retro. 8-bit is the sweet spot for the authentic Streets of Rage sound.
⬛ Max crunch (4-bit)Clean (12-bit) ⬜
8-bit — Classic Genesis (Authentic)
Cut the High Frequencies
The old game hardware couldn't play crispy high sounds — it had a ceiling. Turn this down to make your audio sound muffled and boxy, like it's coming out of a tiny speaker inside a 90s arcade board.
🔇 Muffled / boxyBright 🔊
3,000 Hz — Authentic hardware ceiling
Retro Level (Sample Rate)
Think of this as how many "snapshots" of the audio are taken per second. The fewer snapshots, the more blocky and vintage it sounds. Streets of Rage voices were mostly at 11,025.
Background Hiss
The old chip hardware always had a faint electrical hiss — a bit like a cassette tape. A little bit adds authenticity. Too much sounds like a broken radio.
🔕 SilentHissy 📻
Subtle hiss — Authentic level
How Loud Going In
Boosts the audio before processing begins. Push it higher and the signal will clip and distort as it's crunched — which can actually sound great for punchy, aggressive voices. Keep it at 1× for a clean pass.
🔉 QuieterBoosted / may distort 🔥
1.0× — Normal
Convert to Mono
Original game voices are single-channel (mono) — not stereo. Turn this on and your left/right channels will be blended into one, which is the most authentic result for voice sounds.
Blend stereo → single channel (Mono)
